Methodology

How it actually works — in plain language

We start with Singapore GHS 2025 — the government's official count of who lives where, earns what, and lives how. We know the totals: 74.2% Chinese, 51% female, 78.7% HDB, median income $4,534. But the government doesn't publish every combination (e.g., "30-year-old Malay female in Tampines earning $5,000 in a 4-room HDB").

So we reconstruct the full picture using the same mathematical algorithms that the US Census Bureau, UK ONS, and ETH Zurich use. IPF fills in the cross-tabulations. Bayesian Networks add education, income, housing. Copula adds personality. The result: 20,000 synthetic people, each statistically realistic, none real.

Then we give each synthetic person a "mind" — an LLM reads their demographic profile + Big Five personality and answers your survey question as that person would. Stanford proved this works (Park 2023, Argyle 2023). We're the first to do it on a Census-calibrated population.

The mathematical control is the key: at every step, we measure the error against Census targets. If any dimension drifts >10%, the system auto-corrects. The final population passes 9 out of 9 quality gates, with errors as low as 0.0000 (housing = perfect match).

P
7-Step Synthesis Pipeline
1

IPF 4D Joint Distribution

Take 4 Census marginals (age, gender, ethnicity, planning area) and reconstruct the full 4-dimensional cross-tabulation. 21 age groups x 2 genders x 4 ethnicities x 28 areas = 4,704 cells. Each cell tells us "how many people of type X live in area Y."
Input: 4 marginal distributions from GHS 2025 Method: Deming-Stephan IPF, 200 iterations, convergence threshold 1e-10 Output: 4,704-cell joint frequency table Round: Controlled Rounding (preserves integer counts + marginal totals)
Math guarantee: After convergence, every marginal sum exactly matches Census totals. This is not an approximation — it is a mathematical identity enforced by the algorithm.
SRMSE = 0.0053 (age) SRMSE = 0.0015 (gender) SRMSE = 0.0034 (ethnicity) SRMSE = 0.0075 (area)
2

Bayesian Network — Dependent Attributes

For each of the 20,000 agents, assign education, income, marital status, and residency status. These are NOT random — they follow conditional probabilities from Census data. A 25-year-old gets a different income distribution than a 55-year-old. A university graduate gets a different income than a primary school leaver.
P(education | age_group) — from Census Table 18 P(income | education, age) — from MOM + Census Table 35 P(marital | age, gender) — from Census Table 24 P(residency | age, ethnicity) — from MOM Manpower Reports Method: Conditional Probability Tables (CPT) + weighted random draw
Math guarantee: Each CPT is derived from real cross-tabulations. The dependency structure is preserved — not assumed independent.
2b

MarginalCalibrator — Post-Calibration

After Step 2, some dimensions may drift from Census targets (e.g., university graduates might be 40.8% instead of target 33%). The Calibrator identifies the smallest set of agents to swap, correcting the distribution with minimum disruption to other attributes.
For each calibrated attribute y: |P_synth(y) - P_census(y)| must be < 1/N Algorithm: Minimum-disruption swap 1. Identify over-represented category 2. Find agents where swapping causes least collateral damage 3. Swap only those agents 4. Verify all marginals still hold
Math guarantee: Post-calibration, Education (Degree+ = 33.0%) and Housing (HDB = 78.7%) exactly match Census targets. SRMSE = 0.0001 (education), 0.0000 (housing).
Education: 40.8% -> 33.0% Housing: SRMSE = 0.0000
3

Gaussian Copula — Personality & Behavioral Traits

Generate correlated Big Five personality scores (Openness, Conscientiousness, Extraversion, Agreeableness, Neuroticism) plus risk appetite and social trust. The Copula preserves inter-trait correlations — e.g., high Conscientiousness correlates with low Neuroticism, as observed in SE Asian populations.
SE Asia baseline (Schmitt et al. 2007, 56 nations, N=17,837): O=3.45, C=3.30, E=3.20, A=3.55, N=2.85 Method: Gaussian Copula 1. Generate correlated uniform random variables (from correlation matrix) 2. Transform to target marginal distributions 3. Adjust means/variances by ethnicity, age, gender
Why it matters: Personality drives survey responses. A high-Openness agent is more receptive to new products. A high-Neuroticism agent worries more about risk. Without personality, all 30-year-old Chinese males would answer identically.
4

Rule Engine — Life Phase, Health, NS, Commute

Deterministic rules assign life phase (10 stages from Dependence to End-of-Life), NS status (Singapore males 18-20), health status (age-based probability), and commute mode. These are based on Singapore-specific policy rules (CPF withdrawal age, NS duration, retirement age).
Life phases: Dependence(0-5) -> Growth(6-11) -> Adolescence(12-17) -> NS(18-20,male) -> Establishment(18-34) -> Bearing(25-49) -> Release(50-59) -> Retirement(60-74) -> Decline(75-84) -> End(85+)
5

Household Builder — 10-Phase Family Formation

Group 20,000 individuals into realistic households. This is the hardest step — you can't just randomly assign people. Married couples must have compatible ages. Children must live with parents. Elderly may live alone. The algorithm runs 10 phases, calibrated to Singapore's TFR of ~1.1.
GHS 2025 target household size distribution: 1p=15.3%, 2p=18.8%, 3p=18.0%, 4p=22.3%, 5p=15.5%, 6+=10.1% Strategy E (innovation): Problem: Too many 4-person households after initial formation Solution: Split surplus 4p -> 3p + 1p, then attach freed singles to remaining 4p to form 5p households Result: SRMSE reduced from 0.15 to 0.0495
Math guarantee: After Strategy E + re-calibration, household size distribution matches Census within SRMSE = 0.0495. Housing types are re-calibrated to ensure HDB = 78.7% at household level.
SRMSE = 0.0495 (household size) TFR calibrated to ~1.1
6

K-Anonymity Check

Verify that no combination of attributes uniquely identifies fewer than k=5 agents. This ensures the synthetic population cannot be used to re-identify real individuals — even though no real person's data was ever used.
For every combination of (age_group, gender, ethnicity, area, housing): count(agents matching combination) >= 5 If any cell has < 5: merge adjacent age groups or areas until k >= 5
7

Quality Gate — SRMSE Validation

The final checkpoint. Every dimension is measured against Census targets using SRMSE (Voas & Williamson 2001) — the international standard for synthetic population validation. Hard gates must pass (<0.10). Soft gates should pass (<0.20). All 9 pass.
SRMSE = sqrt( sum((p_i - q_i)^2 / q_i) / K ) where p_i = synthetic proportion, q_i = Census target, K = number of categories Threshold: SRMSE < 0.10 = HARD PASS, < 0.20 = SOFT PASS

M
Mathematical Guarantees
Layer 1 — IPF Marginal Constraint (Step 1): For every dimension d: Sum_cells(d) = Census_target(d) This is an exact equality, not an approximation. Layer 2 — Calibrator Post-Correction (Step 2b): |P_synth(attribute) - P_census(attribute)| < 1/N Minimum-disruption: swap fewest agents necessary. Layer 3 — Quality Gate (Step 7): Hard Gates: SRMSE < 0.10 OR relative_deviation < 10% Soft Gates: SRMSE < 0.20 OR relative_deviation < 20% Result: 9/9 PASS (best: 0.0000, worst: 0.0495) Layer 4 — Privacy (Step 6): K-anonymity: k >= 5 for all attribute combinations Zero real data used. 100% synthetic.
